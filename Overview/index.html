Project overview

Single reference for the P6 industrial prototype and RFP structure.

The P6 is the newest prototype for the Delijuice industrial machines. We are prototyping the 4 extraction module version first, the P6-870, and the 3 larger extraction module P6-970 will be prototyped later. The goal of the project is to validate the design and manufacturing process for the P6-870 and test it in a pilot factory in JiangXi, China in November of 2026. The P6-870 is a 4 extraction module machine with a 280 fruits/min capacity. It is designed to be a compact and efficient machine for small to medium-sized businesses. It can be substituted for the JBT style machines in a standard citrus processing plant.

What P6 is

P6 is the current industrial prototype (Delijuice 870/970). Newest version; prototyping in China with design work via Jamieson. Extractors, deflectors, screw conveyors. Machine lineage: P3 → P4 → P5 (Brazil 2002, Sumitomo cyclo refs) → P6 (current). 323 = commercial/countertop (separate).

  • Jiangxi (JX): Factory with LTA (Long Tai An). Miniplant: 8,160 L/h, 6 extractors, turbo-filter, pasteurizer. LTA pressure for operations by October.
  • Drawings target: April 1st for Sean/YES fabrication (China, Shenzhen area).

General design, manufacturing, and food-safety requirements

Cross-cutting scope for all subsystems (RFP scope).

Materials, food safety, and surfaces

  • Every component must have a specified material. Choose materials to suit function, load, environment (including food and splash zones), and cost. Document material per part or subassembly in each subsystem RFP.
  • Use food-safe materials in all food and splash zones (e.g. stainless steels and compatible polymers); avoid galvanic pairs and corrosive finishes.
  • In food zones target surface roughness around Ra 0.8–1.6 μm so surfaces are cleanable and do not trap pulp.
  • Follow NSF-style guidance: no shelves, pockets, or hidden areas in food or splash zones; all food and liquid must have a defined path to drain.
  • Seal or weld all seams in food and splash zones so there are no capillary gaps; avoid lap joints and unsealed overlaps in these areas.
  • Deburr all edges; no sharp edges or burrs exposed to operators or in any zone that can collect food.

Manufacturing method and manufacturability

For each part, decide the manufacturing method (e.g. laser/plasma cut, bent sheet, machining, casting, weldment) and optimize for manufacturability. Consider complexity, volume, and tolerances. Prefer stock sizes and simple operations where possible.

  • Favour symmetric parts and avoid unnecessary handedness where possible.
  • Minimise machining and secondary processing by using stock sizes, simple weldments, and laser/plasma-cut profiles.

Fasteners and joints

  • All fasteners must be selected (type, size, grade) and the assembly process for every subassembly and the full machine must be defined. Minimise variety of fastener sizes; where practical restrict to M3, M5, M10, and M16 for increasing strength levels. Design for clear access, tooling, and repeatable torque.
  • Avoid exposed threads in food and splash zones; use standoffs, weld studs, or through-holes with nuts outside the zone.
  • Prefer dowel pins for locating parts in shear; use shoulder bolts for joints that carry shear and rotation.
  • Minimise total fastener count with shared brackets and clear load paths; design for easy disassembly and reassembly for cleaning.

Tolerancing

Tolerancing between components is critical for fit, alignment, and bearing life. Specify tolerances that are sufficient for function and agree with manufacturers how they will be achieved (process capability, inspection, rework). Document critical interfaces in each subsystem.

Post-processing

Select post-processing (e.g. grinding, honing, polishing, deburring, passivation) where needed to achieve surface finish, dimensions, or food-safety requirements. Specify per part or zone; manufacturers may propose methods that meet the spec.
